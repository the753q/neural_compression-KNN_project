\begin{abstract}
Neural networks can achieve high levels of compression by learning to represent an image through its underlying structure and producing a size-efficient coding. We employ convolutional autoencoders to extract the bottleneck features as the compressed representation.
\end{abstract}


\begin{IEEEkeywords}
learned image compression, convolutional autoencoders, deep neural networks
\end{IEEEkeywords}


\section{Introduction}
As the availability of processing power rises, so does the volume and resolution of image data. To store these large amounts of data in a size-efficient manner, an ongoing tradeoff between the source file's original structure and its lossy compressed representation has to be made.

Traditional image compression methods, like JPEG, rely on the hand-crafted encoder/decoder block \cite{cheng_deep_2018}. These methods, while possibly not optimal, were also designed to work within the low processing power of older devices.

With the advent of deep learning, new methods of compression have appeared. Instead of having to design the codec using concrete rules, whether programmatically or by hand, a neural network can learn these rules. Without being constrained, the right model architecture can subsequently provide a more efficient representation of an image in the outputted bottleneck features. 

It's important to note, however, that the stronger the compression we require, the bigger the model must be if we want to preserve a high quality representation. The model's weights must also be available on the device that aims to perform the operation. Model inference also requires more power than rule-based methods do.

When it comes to general autoencoder models, the encoding part produces the bottleneck features representing a compressed image, but to decompress, the same neural network operations generally cannot be reversed. As such, the model is built with a second decoder block. During training, the encoder and decoder parts are then evaluated and updated together.


\section{Related Work}
In this section, we review prior research in the field of learned image compression. We summarize existing approaches and key trends in the field, highlighting their underlying assumptions and reported performance characteristics.

\subsection{Convolutional Autoencoder Compression}

Researchers \cite{cheng_deep_2018} built a symmetric convolutional autoencoder structure with multiple downsampling and upsampling units to generate feature maps with low dimensions. The encoder part was a pipeline transforming the source RGB image to YCbCr format and normalizing it to the range $[0,1]$. The image then went into the CAE network block, which encapsulated both the encoder and decoder pipelines. The CAE itself comprised 3 downsampling units in the first part, each consisting of 2 convolutional layers. Each convolution was followed by a PReLU activation. During training, uniform noise was added to the latent vector, imitating the effect of the quantizer present at inference time, which was not differentiable and thus could not be used directly during training. An approximated rate-distortion was used as the loss function:
\begin{equation}
    J(\theta, \phi; x) = \|x - \hat{x}\|^2 + \lambda \cdot \|y\|^2
    \label{eq:cae_loss}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\|x - \hat{x}\|^2$ was the MSE between the original $x$ 
and the reconstructed $\hat{x} = g_\phi(f_\theta(x) + \mu)$ image, with $\mu$ being the 
uniform noise. 
The term $\|y\|^2 = \|f_\theta(x)\|^2$ penalized the amplitude of the latent vector, 
acting as a proxy for the bitrate. The scalar $\lambda$ controlled the rate-distortion 
tradeoff. The model was optimized using Adam with a fixed learning rate of $10^{-4}$. The output of the pre-trained CAE utilized a PCA rotation to produce more zeros, improving efficiency. Subsequently, 12-bit quantization and the entropy coder were used to compress the data further. The model itself always expected a 128x128x1 tensor as input and outputted a 16x16x32 feature map. To achieve compression on larger image sizes, they sliced the images into patches and ran the CAE independently on each slice. Their results outperformed conventional image coding algorithms and achieved a 13.7\% BD-rate saving compared to JPEG2000 on the Kodak dataset. Their average running time, however, was 80 ms longer on GPU and 1700 ms longer on CPU than with JPEG2000. 


\section{Evaluation}
We describe the datasets used for training our compression models and the ways in which we evaluate them.

\subsection{Datasets}
In this work, we aim to train a compression model for common images. With this in mind, our models have to handle pictures taken with consumer smartphones. As such, the chosen datasets contain images with resolutions of at least 400 pixels on average. Additionally, the datasets must contain enough pictures to prevent overfitting of the models.

\paragraph{ImageNet 10k}
ImageNet is an image dataset organized according to concepts constructed from the WordNet hierarchy. Each concept category is, on average, represented by about 1000 images \cite{noauthor_imagenet_nodate-1}.

For basic experiments, we use a subset of the dataset. The ImageNet 10k dataset contains 10 randomly selected images from each of the 1000 classes of the original \cite{noauthor_imagenet_nodate}. 

\paragraph{Div2K High Resolution Images}
The Div2K is a large dataset with a diverse range of contents. It is split into various categories of image quality, of which we only use the high resolution subset (of around 2k resolution) \cite{noauthor_div2k_nodate}. 

\paragraph{Flickr2K}
2650 high-quality images from Flickr, commonly used for super-resolution training. We use it combined with Div2K; the combination is sometimes called DF2K \cite{noauthor_flickr2k_nodate}. 

\paragraph{Flickr-Faces-HQ small}
The Flickr Faces dataset contains high-quality images of human faces. It offers 70,000 PNG images at 1024×1024 resolution that display diverse ages, ethnicities, image backgrounds, and accessories like hats and eyeglasses \cite{noauthor_flickr-faces-hq_nodate}.

\subsection{Metrics}
We evaluate the models based on various metrics, such as compression strength, reconstruction quality, and compute cost.

\paragraph{Bits per Pixel}
The bits per pixel (BPP) metric is defined as the ratio of the total size of the compressed image to the total number of pixels in the image. To calculate it, we can define it as \cite{ungureanu_image-compression_2024}:

\begin{equation}
    \text{BPP} = \frac{\text{file size in bits}}{H \times W}
\end{equation}
where $H$ and $W$ is the height and weight of the image.

Comparing the BPP values of the source and compressed file gives us the compression strength.

\paragraph{Mean Squared Error}
Mean squared error (MSE) captures the pixel-wise squared difference averaged \cite{ungureanu_image-compression_2024}: 

\begin{equation}
    \text{MSE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio}
Peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR) is defined as the ratio of the maximum pixel intensity to the mean-square error, and it is measured in decibels (dB). A higher value for the PSNR metric represents better image quality \cite{ungureanu_image-compression_2024}:
\begin{equation}
    \text{PSNR} = 10 \cdot \log_{10} \left( \frac{MAX_I^2}{MSE} \right)
\end{equation}

where $MAX_I$ represents the maximum possible pixel value of the image. Compared to MSE, PSNR transforms the error scale into a more manageable logarithmic one, where in practice, most values land on a scale of $[0, 100]$ (theoretically, in the case of no error, to $\infty$).

\paragraph{Structure Similarity Index}
Structural similarity index (SSIM) measures structural similarity. It models perceived quality by comparing local luminance, contrast, and structural correlation, reflecting the non-linear sensitivity of the human visual system to image distortions \cite{cheng_deep_2018}. It's based on visible structures inside the image and can predict the perceived quality of the compressed image. 
The resulting value of this metric is between -1 and 1 (when identical). The equation is:

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{SSIM}(x,y) =
\frac{(2\mu_x \mu_y + C_1)(2\sigma_{xy} + C_2)}
     {(\mu_x^2 + \mu_y^2 + C_1)(\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_y^2 + C_2)}
\end{equation}

where $\mu_x$ and $\mu_y$ are the local means of windows $x$ and $y$, 
$\sigma_x^2$ and $\sigma_y^2$ are the corresponding variances, 
$\sigma_{xy}$ is the covariance between $x$ and $y$, 
$C_1 = (K_1 L)^2$ and $C_2 = (K_2 L)^2$ are stability constants, 
$L$ is the dynamic range of pixel values (for 8-bit images, 255), 
and $K_1$, $K_2$ are small constants (typically $K_1=0.01$ and $K_2=0.03$) \cite{ungureanu_image-compression_2024}.

\section{Experiments}
This section explains the structure of our work and the experiments conducted to evaluate its compression strength.

\subsection{Workflow and Implementation Details}
For ease of use, we propose a harness that allows for seamless swapping of different compression architectures. The harness provides standardized dataset loading and preprocessing while also providing evaluation capabilities.

First, the $data.py$ file defines the various datasets we use, each as its own subclass of the PyTorch Lightning \cite{noauthor_pytorch_nodate} library $LightningDataModule$, achieving seamless integration within the main training code. Batch size and the number of workers for batch preparation are defined for each module. Additionally, we set whether we want the images to be scaled to a fixed size or cut into patches. By splitting each image into sub-parts, we allow the model to learn from more examples and prevent overfitting. Multiple datasets can be concatenated as well. Inside the class, we also define the separate train, test, and validation sets. 

The $train.py$ file describes the main training pipeline, which contains experiments that combine datasets with models. PyTorch Lightning then takes care of the training process.

Models are built on top of a base autoencoder module in the $base.py$ file, implementing training and validation steps, all built on top of $LightningModule$. The concrete model architecture is then implemented in its own $.py$ file, inheriting the $BaseAutoencoder$ class and usually implementing the encoder, decoder, and forward pass components of the model.

Evaluation is then performed via the $evaluate.py$ file, which loads dataset modules, compression metrics, and performs the compression and decompression of images with a specified trained model. 
Each image is sliced into parts, with each part compressed individually. After subsequent decompression, the slices are merged together to reconstruct the original image. The slicing algorithm creates non-overlapping slices that, during compression, leave the model without the context of the surrounding area.

Based on the decompressed image, the MSE, PSNR, and SSIM metrics are calculated. We also compare our method to JPEG compression in terms of image quality and compression rate.

\subsection{Current State}

We have currently implemented the architecture from \cite{cheng_deep_2018} as closely as possible based on the description in the paper, with some temporary simplifications. In short, we work with normalized YCbCr images and process each channel separately, so a 128×128×1 patch is the input to the encoder. We perform PCA rotations, then apply a simple quantization formula as mentioned in the paper. We quantize the 3 latent vectors, one per channel in YCbCr, as well as 3 rotation matrices from PCA. This quantized data are then encoded using Huffman coding. The entropy method is temporary and may be replaced later; currently, we are focusing on producing low-entropy latents after the PCA rotation.

Current PCA, quantization pipeline \cite{cheng_deep_2018}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \hfill $\vphantom{\dfrac{y'}{2^{B-1}}} y_{\mathrm{rot}} = U^T y$ \hfill\null
    \item \hfill $\vphantom{\dfrac{y'}{2^{B-1}}} y' = \left\lfloor 2^{B-1} \cdot y_{\mathrm{rot}} \right\rceil$ \hfill\null
    \item \hfill $\tilde{y} = \dfrac{y'}{2^{B-1}}$ \hfill\null
    \item \hfill $\vphantom{\dfrac{y'}{2^{B-1}}} \hat{y} = U\tilde{y}$ \hfill\null
\end{enumerate}
Here $y$ is the latent vector, encoder output; $\hat{y}$ is the recovered latent that will go into the decoder network; $U$ is the PCA matrix produced from the image feature maps; and $B$ is the desired number of bits.

We plan to conduct more experiments with this model, including:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Train on the DF2K dataset with many more epochs.
    \item Train with different distortion losses: MAE, Huber.
    \item Train with different rate loss: $-\sum{x \cdot log_2{(x)}}$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Base model}
In this paper, we describe our attempt at reimplementing the existing work of a CAE-based architecture \cite{cheng_deep_2018}. Now, we report the achieved results.

\paragraph{Patch-Level Evaluation}
To understand the intrinsic reconstruction capabilities of our architecture and the degradation introduced by the compression of the bottleneck vector step, we first evaluated the model on $128 \times 128$ image patches. This evaluation was performed over a validation set of 50 batches. 
We compared two specific configurations: reconstruction with and without the bottleneck compression.
The quantitative results are summarized in Table ~\ref{tab:patch_metrics}.

\subsection{Discussion}
Our evaluation demonstrates that the proposed implementation achieves distinctive file size reductions. On average, the compression factor ranges from 2.9x to 4.9x. For several images, our model achieves a higher compression ratio than the JPEG baseline at a quality level of 95. 

However, the implemented architecture fails when compared to the JPEG baseline, which currently outperforms our approach in terms of standard distortion metrics (PSNR and SSIM). The average PSNR for our model across the dataset is 25.18 dB, compared to 38.57 dB for JPEG.

\section{Future Work}
We aim to explore modeling a size-agnostic architecture for image compression that would work for varying image sizes, with the latent vector changing in size as a result. We consider using a fully convolutional network, which would be trained on smaller slices of, for example, 256x256 pixels, to make the model learn compression quickly on a large number of examples. During inference, images of various sizes would be compressed and examined \cite{balle_end--end_2017} \cite{townsend_hilloc_2019}.

To improve the rate–distortion tradeoff, a hyper-encoder block can be trained to output a hyperprior which, when provided to the entropy coder alongside the latent vector, enables it to differentiate between fine-grained textures and monotone structures, thereby reducing coding entropy and allowing lower distortion at a given bitrate \cite{balle_variational_2018}.

Alternatively, replacing the PCA rotation and quantization stage of our reproduced base model inspired by \cite{cheng_deep_2018} with a vector quantization (VQ) bottleneck \cite{oord_neural_2018} could allow quantization to be optimized jointly with the rest of the model. Furthermore, the PCA rotation would become redundant, as the codebook could learn optimal discrete representations directly. The resulting bitstream would consist of integer codebook indices rather than quantized floats, simplifying the entropy coding stage.

As human vision is more sensitive to changes in brightness than to changes in color, by converting our images to YCbCr format, we could implement chroma subsampling. Full resolution would only be given to the luminance channel, with the chroma channels downsampled \cite{prativadibhayankaram_study_2024}.

Most images contain only a handful of regions of interest. Focusing the processing power on those areas and spending less on the background could yield performance improvements, although with the model architecture rising in complexity \cite{tao_adanic_2023}.
